Research Overview

Athens Digital Glossa Chronos: Advanced research in historical linguistics and language contact

Research Focus Areas

Core research domains and methodological approaches

Historical Linguistics

Diachronic analysis of language change across historical periods, with emphasis on Greek and Indo-European language families.

  • Diachronic syntax and morphology
  • Grammaticalization patterns
  • Language family comparisons
  • Historical corpus analysis
  • Typological change tracking

Language Contact

Systematic study of language contact phenomena through translations and bilingual texts, focusing on Greek-Indo-European interactions.

  • Contact-induced change
  • Translation universals
  • Lexical borrowing patterns
  • Syntactic calques
  • Bilingual corpus analysis

Argument Structure

Advanced analysis of predicate-argument relations and valency patterns in historical languages using computational methods.

  • Valency frame analysis
  • Argument realization patterns
  • Syntactic alternations
  • Diachronic valency change
  • Cross-linguistic comparison

Research Methodology

Computational and corpus-based approaches to historical linguistics

Corpus-Based Analysis

Large-scale corpus analysis using PROIEL treebanks and other annotated historical language resources.

  • 500K+ annotated tokens
  • 15+ historical languages
  • 4 major historical periods
  • Complete morphological annotation
  • Syntactic dependency relations

Computational Methods

Advanced computational techniques for linguistic analysis and pattern recognition in historical data.

  • Dependency parsing algorithms
  • Machine learning applications
  • Statistical modeling
  • Pattern recognition
  • Data visualization

Key Research Questions

Central questions driving our research agenda

Diachronic Change

How do syntactic and morphological patterns change over time, and what factors drive these changes?

  • Mechanisms of grammaticalization
  • Role of language contact in change
  • Typological shifts in syntax
  • Rate and directionality of change
  • Stability vs. innovation patterns

Contact Phenomena

What are the systematic patterns of language contact, and how do they manifest in translations and bilingual texts?

  • Lexical borrowing hierarchies
  • Syntactic calque patterns
  • Morphological transfer
  • Translation universals
  • Contact-induced grammaticalization

Argument Structure

How do argument structure patterns vary across languages and historical periods, and what factors influence this variation?

  • Valency frame stability
  • Argument realization alternatives
  • Cross-linguistic typology
  • Diachronic valency change
  • Semantic vs. syntactic factors

Methodological Innovation

How can computational methods advance historical linguistics research and what new insights can they provide?

  • Large-scale pattern detection
  • Statistical modeling of change
  • Machine learning applications
  • Visualization of diachronic data
  • Automated annotation methods

Publications and Outputs

Recent research outputs and academic contributions

Language Contact in Biblical Translations: A Computational Approach

Nikolaos Lavidas, Maria Papadopoulou, Andreas Schmidt
Journal of Historical Linguistics, 2026
2026
This study presents a computational analysis of language contact phenomena in biblical translations across Greek, Latin, Gothic, and Old Church Slavonic. Using PROIEL treebank data, we identify systematic patterns of lexical borrowing, syntactic calques, and morphological transfer.
DOI: 10.1234/jhl.2026.001

Diachronic Valency Patterns in Greek: From Classical to Modern

Maria Papadopoulou, Nikolaos Lavidas
Greek Linguistics Review, 2025
2025
A comprehensive study of valency pattern changes in Greek across four historical periods. Analysis of 50,000 annotated tokens reveals systematic changes in argument structure, case marking, and syntactic alternations.
DOI: 10.1234/glr.2025.015
